Rocket Yoga

The Dynamic Practice That Gets You There Faster

Quick Answer

Rocket Yoga is a dynamic, fast-paced practice created by Larry Schultz in 1980s San Francisco that rearranges traditional Ashtanga sequences to make advanced poses more accessible. Named because "it gets you there faster," Rocket emphasizes arm balances, inversions, modifications, and personal expression while maintaining the heat-building, breath-synchronized movement of its Ashtanga roots.

Rocket Yoga lands somewhere between rebellion and reverence. It takes the rigorous structure of Ashtanga, cracks it open, and invites you to play inside. Created by a teacher who loved the traditional practice but questioned its gatekeeping, Rocket offers the intensity and heat of its parent lineage with a distinctly American twist: accessibility, creativity, and a willingness to break the rules in service of the practice itself.

The name comes from a simple observation. When Larry Schultz began teaching his modified sequences in San Francisco, students noticed they were progressing faster than in traditional Ashtanga. The practice, they said, "gets you there faster." Like a rocket. The name stuck, and it captures something essential about the style: this is yoga with momentum, yoga that builds on itself, yoga that wants to take you somewhere.

But "faster" doesn't mean "easier." Rocket classes are challenging, often featuring arm balances and inversions that would appear late in the Ashtanga series, if at all. The difference is in the approach. Where traditional Ashtanga requires mastery of each pose before moving forward, Rocket says: try it now. Modify as needed. Fall. Learn. The destination matters less than the willingness to move toward it.

"It's called Rocket because it gets you there faster." - Larry Schultz, creator of Rocket Yoga

Origins & History

Larry Schultz was not your typical yoga teacher. A dedicated student of Sri K. Pattabhi Jois in Mysore, India, he completed advanced series and became one of the few Western practitioners authorized to teach Ashtanga in the 1980s. But Schultz was also a product of San Francisco's counterculture, a man who saw the rigid hierarchy of traditional yoga and wondered if there was another way.

When he returned to San Francisco, Schultz opened a studio called It's Yoga and began teaching to a diverse population that included touring rock musicians from the Grateful Dead (he would become their personal yoga teacher). His students weren't interested in waiting years to attempt challenging poses. They wanted to move, to sweat, to challenge themselves immediately. Schultz responded by reimagining the sequences.

He kept the fundamental structure: sun salutations, standing poses, seated poses, finishing sequence. He kept the breath-synchronized movement, the internal heat, the meditative quality of consistent practice. But he rearranged the order, introduced poses from advanced series earlier, and most importantly, he opened the door to modification and personal expression. If you couldn't do a pose, you tried a variation. If you wanted to add something, you added it. The practice became collaborative between teacher and student.

Larry Schultz believed that yoga should be accessible to everyone, not reserved for those who could dedicate decades to mastering a single sequence. Rocket was his answer: all the benefits of Ashtanga, with the gates thrown open.

Schultz taught until his death in 2011, and his legacy continues through teachers he trained directly and through the growing global community of Rocket practitioners. The style has particularly strong roots in the UK, where teachers like David Kyle have expanded and systematized the practice while maintaining its essential spirit of accessibility and play.

The Three Rocket Sequences

Rocket Yoga is organized into three main routines, each with a distinct character and purpose. Understanding these sequences helps you know what to expect and which classes might suit your current practice.

Rocket I (Rocket Routine One)

Based on Ashtanga's Primary Series, Rocket I focuses on forward folds, hip openers, and foundational strength. It's the most accessible of the three sequences and serves as the foundation for the practice. However, even Rocket I includes arm balances and inversions that would appear later in traditional Ashtanga progression.

  • Emphasis on forward folds and hip opening
  • Introduction to arm balances like crow pose (bakasana)
  • Foundational strength building
  • Accessible modifications throughout

Rocket II (Rocket Routine Two)

Drawing from Ashtanga's Intermediate Series, Rocket II emphasizes backbends, deeper hip openers, and more challenging arm balances. This sequence builds significant spinal flexibility and requires (and develops) greater core strength.

  • Deep backbending sequences
  • Advanced arm balances and transitions
  • Significant core engagement
  • Greater demand on spinal mobility

Rocket III (The Happy Hour)

Often called "The Happy Hour," Rocket III combines elements from both sequences, alternating between Rocket I and Rocket II poses. It's the most creative and playful of the sequences, allowing for significant personal expression and teacher innovation.

  • Fusion of forward folds and backbends
  • Maximum variety and creative sequencing
  • Space for teacher and student expression
  • The most dynamic and unpredictable sequence

Many studios also offer "Rocket Basics" or "Rocket Foundations" classes that introduce the key poses and principles at a slower pace, making the practice accessible to newer students before they dive into the full sequences.

What to Expect in a Rocket Yoga Class

Walking into your first Rocket class, you'll notice the energy immediately. The room is typically warm (though not as hot as Bikram), and there's often music playing - a departure from the silence of traditional Ashtanga. The vibe tends to be encouraging and slightly irreverent, reflecting the style's San Francisco roots.

Dynamic Warm-Up

Classes begin with sun salutations (Surya Namaskar A and B), building internal heat quickly. Expect multiple rounds, with the pace faster than in gentle or beginner classes. The breath drives everything.

Arm Balances Throughout

Unlike traditional Ashtanga where arm balances appear later, Rocket introduces them early and often. Crow pose, side crow, and flying pigeon are common. Modifications are always offered.

Creative Transitions

Movement between poses is emphasized as much as the poses themselves. You might flow from headstand to chaturanga, or jump through from downward dog. The transitions build strength and keep the practice dynamic.

Modifications Welcome

This is key to Rocket's identity. You're encouraged to modify any pose, skip what doesn't serve you, and add variations that feel right. The teacher offers options, not mandates.

Inversions Featured

Headstand, forearm stand, and handstand work appear in most classes. Wall space may be used for practice. Teachers generally offer progressions so everyone can participate at their level.

Music & Energy

Many Rocket classes feature music, ranging from ambient beats to more energetic tracks. The atmosphere is often playful and encouraging, with teachers offering verbal motivation throughout.

Class Structure

A typical 75-90 minute Rocket class follows this general structure:

  • Opening (5-10 minutes): Centering, breath awareness, setting intention
  • Sun Salutations (10-15 minutes): Multiple rounds of Surya Namaskar A and B to build heat
  • Standing Sequence (15-20 minutes): Standing poses with balances and initial arm balance attempts
  • Primary/Seated Work (20-25 minutes): Floor work varying by which Rocket sequence is being taught
  • Backbends or Hip Openers (10-15 minutes): Deeper work depending on the sequence
  • Finishing Sequence (10-15 minutes): Inversions, cool-down, and savasana

Rocket sits at an interesting intersection: it maintains the structure and intensity of Ashtanga while incorporating the creativity and accessibility of modern vinyasa. If you love Ashtanga but find the traditional progression frustrating, or if you want more challenge than a typical flow class offers, Rocket may be your ideal practice.

6 Key Benefits of Rocket Yoga

Why practitioners are drawn to this dynamic style

01

Builds Functional Strength

The arm balances, inversions, and dynamic transitions develop practical strength that translates to daily activities. You build muscle through movement, not isolation.

02

Accelerates Flexibility

The combination of heat, breath, and consistent practice opens the body efficiently. Many practitioners see faster progress than in gentler styles.

03

Develops Mental Focus

Balancing on your hands demands presence. There's no room for mental wandering when you're upside down. The practice trains concentration naturally.

04

Builds Confidence

Attempting poses that once seemed impossible, falling, and trying again creates resilience. The practice teaches that failure is part of progress.

05

Encourages Personal Expression

Unlike rigid styles, Rocket invites creativity. You develop your own relationship with the poses rather than conforming to a single ideal.

06

Creates Community

The playful, encouraging atmosphere of Rocket classes often builds strong community bonds. Students support each other through challenging sequences.

Who Is Rocket Yoga For?

Rocket welcomes anyone willing to work, but certain practitioners tend to find particular connection with the practice.

Ashtanga Students Seeking Variety

If you love Ashtanga's structure but want more creative freedom, Rocket offers the best of both worlds.

Athletes & Cross-Trainers

The strength-building aspects and high intensity appeal to those who want yoga that challenges them physically.

Intermediate Practitioners Ready to Progress

If you've mastered basics and want to tackle inversions and arm balances, Rocket provides the pathway.

Those Who Get Bored Easily

The dynamic nature and creative variations keep the practice fresh. No two classes feel exactly the same.

Community Seekers

The supportive, playful atmosphere of Rocket classes often creates strong bonds between practitioners.

Creative Spirits

If you prefer exploration over prescription, Rocket's emphasis on personal expression resonates deeply.

Who Might Want to Start Elsewhere

While Rocket is more accessible than traditional Ashtanga, complete beginners may want to build some foundation first. Consider starting with:

  • Vinyasa basics if you're new to flow-style yoga
  • Hatha yoga if you need more time in poses to understand alignment
  • Rocket Foundations/Basics classes if your studio offers them

Those with wrist injuries or conditions affecting the shoulders may need significant modifications, as arm balances feature prominently. That said, good Rocket teachers excel at offering alternatives that maintain the spirit of the practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to know Ashtanga before trying Rocket?

No, prior Ashtanga experience isn't required. However, some yoga experience is helpful. Rocket moves quickly and assumes familiarity with basic poses like downward dog, warrior positions, and chaturanga. If you're completely new to yoga, consider taking a few beginner vinyasa or hatha classes first, or look for "Rocket Basics" classes at your studio.

What if I can't do arm balances or inversions?

Then you're in exactly the right place to learn. Rocket's philosophy embraces the attempt more than the achievement. Good teachers offer progressions: crow pose begins with feet on blocks, headstand starts at the wall, handstand begins with hops. You're never expected to do everything - you're expected to try and to modify as needed. That's the whole point.

Is Rocket the same as Power Yoga?

They're related but distinct. Both emerged from Ashtanga in the 1980s-90s and both are physically demanding. However, Rocket maintains closer ties to its Ashtanga roots with three defined sequences, consistent pose order, and traditional breath techniques. Power Yoga is generally more fitness-focused and varies more widely between teachers. Think of Rocket as a specific system, while Power Yoga is more of a category.

How often should I practice Rocket?

This depends on your fitness level and recovery capacity. Many dedicated practitioners do 4-6 sessions weekly, but 2-3 sessions produce significant results for most people. Because of its intensity, allow at least one recovery day between sessions when starting out. Listen to your body - more isn't always better. Quality and consistency matter more than frequency.

What should I bring to a Rocket class?

Bring a yoga mat with good grip (you'll sweat), a towel, and water. Wear fitted clothing that won't fall over your head during inversions. Avoid eating for 2-3 hours before class. Props like blocks and straps are helpful for modifications, though most studios provide them. Some practitioners bring their own for hygiene reasons.

Which Rocket sequence should I start with?

Most teachers recommend starting with Rocket I, which focuses on forward folds and foundational strength. It's considered the most accessible of the three sequences. Once you're comfortable with Rocket I, try Rocket II for its backbending emphasis, then Rocket III for the creative fusion of both. However, any good teacher will offer modifications regardless of which sequence the class follows.

Can I practice Rocket if I have injuries?

It depends on the injury. Wrist and shoulder issues require careful modification since arm balances are prominent. Back injuries may preclude some backbends in Rocket II. The key is communication: tell your teacher before class about any injuries or limitations. Rocket's emphasis on modification means good teachers can usually adapt the practice. For serious injuries, consult a healthcare provider and consider gentler styles during recovery.

Is there a Rocket teacher certification?

Yes, several organizations offer Rocket Yoga teacher training. Larry Schultz's lineage continues through teachers he trained directly, and organizations like It's Yoga and Rocket Yoga UK offer certifications. When seeking a Rocket teacher, look for those who have trained specifically in the system rather than general yoga teachers who offer "rocket-style" classes. The depth of understanding makes a significant difference.
