26+2 Hot Yoga

The original heated yoga sequence: 26 postures and 2 breathing exercises in 105 degrees

Quick Answer

26+2 Hot Yoga is a fixed-sequence practice featuring 26 postures and 2 breathing exercises, performed in a room heated to 105 degrees Fahrenheit (40 degrees Celsius) with 40% humidity. Originally known as Bikram Yoga, this style follows the same sequence in every 90-minute class, allowing practitioners to track their progress and deepen their practice over time.

The heat warms your muscles for safer stretching, promotes detoxification through sweating, and creates an intensely focused environment. Whether you are seeking physical transformation, mental discipline, or both, this practice delivers a challenging and rewarding experience.

The first thing you notice is the heat. You walk into the studio and feel the warm air wrap around you like a blanket. Within minutes, you are sweating. This is 26+2 Hot Yoga: a practice designed to challenge your body and focus your mind in ways that cooler environments simply cannot.

Developed in the 1970s by Bikram Choudhury, this sequence was originally called Bikram Yoga and spread to thousands of studios worldwide. Following controversies surrounding its founder, many studios have rebranded their offerings as "26+2 Hot Yoga," "Original Hot Yoga," or simply "Hot 26." The practice itself, however, remains unchanged: the same 26 postures and 2 breathing exercises, performed in the same order, in the same heated conditions that made this style famous.

What makes this practice unique is not just the heat, but the consistency. Unlike vinyasa or other flow styles where sequences vary, 26+2 follows an identical sequence in every class. This repetition is intentional. When the sequence never changes, you become acutely aware of what does change: your flexibility, your strength, your stamina, your mental clarity. The practice becomes a mirror, reflecting your progress back to you class after class.

"The heated room is not about making yoga harder. It is about creating conditions where your body can work more safely and more deeply. The heat is medicine for the muscles."

Students often describe their first class as humbling. The heat is intense, the postures demanding, and the 90 minutes feel like a journey unto themselves. But those who return find something remarkable: the practice that once seemed impossible becomes possible, then comfortable, then deeply rewarding. This is the promise of 26+2: a practice that meets you wherever you are and grows with you over years and decades.

What to Expect in Class

Walking into your first 26+2 class can feel overwhelming. Understanding what happens before, during, and after class will help you prepare mentally and physically for this unique experience.

The Heat

The room is heated to 105 degrees Fahrenheit (40 degrees Celsius) with 40% humidity. This environment warms muscles quickly, allowing for deeper stretches while promoting intense sweating. Expect to be drenched within the first 15 minutes.

Hydration Essentials

Bring at least 32 ounces of water, though many students bring more. Hydrate well in the hours before class, not just during. Electrolyte drinks can help replenish what you lose through sweating. This is non-negotiable for a safe practice.

Towels and Gear

You will need a large towel to cover your entire mat, as it will become slippery with sweat. A hand towel for wiping your face is also essential. Wear minimal, moisture-wicking clothing: shorts and a sports bra or tank top work well.

Mirror Practice

Unlike many yoga styles, 26+2 is practiced facing a mirror. You are encouraged to watch yourself throughout the sequence. The mirror provides instant feedback on alignment and helps develop body awareness and concentration.

Teacher Dialogue

Teachers follow a specific dialogue, giving the same instructions in each class. This scripted approach ensures consistency and allows students to focus entirely on the practice rather than processing new information each time.

90-Minute Format

Traditional classes run 90 minutes. The first half consists of standing postures performed twice each, while the second half moves to the floor for seated, prone, and supine poses. Brief resting periods separate the more challenging sequences.

Breathing Exercises

Class begins with Pranayama (Standing Deep Breathing) to oxygenate the blood and warm the body. It ends with Kapalabhati (Blowing in Firm), a powerful breathing exercise that energizes and cleanses. These bookend breathing practices are essential to the sequence.

Physical Intensity

Expect your heart rate to elevate significantly. The combination of heat and physical exertion creates a cardiovascular challenge. Beginners should pace themselves, take breaks when needed, and never push through dizziness or nausea.

The Complete 26+2 Sequence

Standing Series

  • 1 Pranayama (Standing Deep Breathing)
  • 2 Ardha Chandrasana (Half Moon Pose)
  • 3 Utkatasana (Awkward Pose)
  • 4 Garurasana (Eagle Pose)
  • 5 Dandayamana Janushirasana (Standing Head to Knee)
  • 6 Dandayamana Dhanurasana (Standing Bow)
  • 7 Tuladandasana (Balancing Stick)
  • 8 Dandayamana Bibhaktapada Paschimottanasana (Standing Separate Leg Stretching)
  • 9 Trikanasana (Triangle Pose)
  • 10 Dandayamana Bibhaktapada Janushirasana (Standing Separate Leg Head to Knee)
  • 11 Tadasana (Tree Pose)
  • 12 Padangustasana (Toe Stand)
  • 13 Savasana (Dead Body Pose - 2 min rest)

Floor Series

  • 14 Pavanamuktasana (Wind Removing Pose)
  • 15 Sit-Up (Transition)
  • 16 Bhujangasana (Cobra Pose)
  • 17 Salabhasana (Locust Pose)
  • 18 Poorna Salabhasana (Full Locust)
  • 19 Dhanurasana (Bow Pose)
  • 20 Supta Vajrasana (Fixed Firm Pose)
  • 21 Ardha Kurmasana (Half Tortoise)
  • 22 Ustrasana (Camel Pose)
  • 23 Sasangasana (Rabbit Pose)
  • 24 Janushirasana/Paschimottanasana (Head to Knee/Stretching)
  • 25 Ardha Matsyendrasana (Spine Twist)
  • 26 Kapalabhati (Blowing in Firm)
  • 27 Final Savasana (Closing Rest)

Benefits of 26+2 Hot Yoga

Consistent practice in the heated room offers transformative physical and mental benefits

01

Enhanced Flexibility

The 105-degree heat warms muscles, tendons, and ligaments rapidly, allowing for deeper stretches than possible at room temperature. Over time, practitioners often achieve flexibility they never thought possible, particularly in the spine, hips, and hamstrings.

02

Cardiovascular Conditioning

Working in heat elevates heart rate significantly, creating cardiovascular benefits similar to moderate aerobic exercise. The heart works harder to cool the body, improving circulation and building endurance over time.

03

Detoxification Through Sweat

Profuse sweating opens pores and helps eliminate toxins through the skin. While the body's primary detox organs are the liver and kidneys, sweating supports the elimination process and leaves practitioners feeling cleansed and refreshed.

04

Mental Discipline

Staying present and focused in extreme heat builds mental resilience that transfers to daily life. The practice teaches you to remain calm under pressure, breathe through discomfort, and find stillness when everything in you wants to flee.

05

Measurable Progress

Because the sequence never changes, you become acutely aware of your improvements. The posture you could not do last month becomes accessible this month. This tangible progress keeps practitioners motivated and engaged over years of practice.

06

Full-Body Strengthening

The 26 postures work every muscle group, joint, and organ in the body. Standing postures build leg strength and balance, while floor series develops core stability and back strength. The practice creates functional, balanced strength.

Who Is 26+2 Hot Yoga For?

Structure Seekers

If you thrive on routine and want to know exactly what to expect each class, the fixed sequence provides comfort and clarity. No surprises, just deep work.

Progress Trackers

Athletes and goal-oriented individuals appreciate the ability to measure improvement. The same sequence reveals your development with undeniable clarity.

Heat Lovers

If you enjoy saunas, steam rooms, or feel most alive in warm environments, the heated room will feel like coming home rather than a challenge to endure.

Mental Discipline Seekers

Those looking to build mental fortitude and stress resilience find the practice transformative. Staying calm in extreme conditions translates to staying calm in life.

Detox Enthusiasts

If you are drawn to practices that promote sweating and physical cleansing, the intense perspiration of 26+2 delivers what other practices cannot.

Committed Beginners

New to yoga but ready for a challenge? The consistency of the sequence means you learn faster. Every class builds on the last in a clear, logical progression.

Consider Another Style If:

  • You have heat intolerance or heat-sensitive medical conditions
  • You are pregnant (consult your doctor and consider prenatal yoga instead)
  • You have uncontrolled high blood pressure or heart conditions
  • You prefer creative, flowing sequences that change each class
  • You have a history of heat stroke or severe dehydration
  • You are looking for a gentle, restorative practice

Preparing for Your First Class

Success in your first 26+2 class depends largely on preparation. Here is how to set yourself up for the best possible experience:

The Day Before

  • Hydrate aggressively. Drink at least 64 ounces of water throughout the day. Add electrolytes if possible. You cannot hydrate enough during class alone.
  • Eat a light dinner. Avoid heavy, greasy, or spicy foods. Your digestive system will thank you in the heat.
  • Get adequate sleep. A tired body struggles more in extreme conditions.

The Day Of

  • Eat 2-3 hours before class. A light meal of easily digestible foods: fruit, toast, or a small smoothie. Avoid dairy and heavy proteins.
  • Continue hydrating but stop drinking large amounts 30 minutes before class to avoid discomfort.
  • Skip the coffee. Caffeine is a diuretic and can contribute to dehydration in the heat.

What to Bring

  • Yoga mat (most studios rent them if needed)
  • Large towel to cover your mat
  • Hand towel for wiping sweat
  • 32+ ounces of water (more is better)
  • Change of clothes for after class
  • Minimal, moisture-wicking clothing to wear

During Class

  • Arrive 15 minutes early to acclimate to the heat before class begins.
  • Set up near the door if you are nervous about the heat.
  • Take breaks when needed. Sitting or lying down is always acceptable.
  • Stay in the room. Even if you rest, the heat continues to work on your body.
  • Focus on your breath. When the heat feels overwhelming, slow, steady breathing is your anchor.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is 26+2 Hot Yoga exactly?

26+2 Hot Yoga is a style of yoga featuring a fixed sequence of 26 postures and 2 breathing exercises, practiced in a room heated to 105 degrees Fahrenheit (40 degrees Celsius) with 40% humidity. Originally known as Bikram Yoga, the practice was developed in the 1970s and follows the exact same sequence in every class. The heat warms muscles for deeper stretching while promoting detoxification through sweating.

Why did studios stop calling it Bikram Yoga?

Following multiple allegations of sexual assault and harassment against founder Bikram Choudhury, and subsequent legal judgments against him, many studios chose to distance themselves from his name while continuing to teach the sequence. Names like "26+2 Hot Yoga," "Original Hot Yoga," and "Hot 26" allow studios to teach the same practice without association with the founder. The sequence itself remains unchanged regardless of what the class is called.

Why is the room heated to 105 degrees?

The specific temperature serves multiple purposes. First, it rapidly warms muscles, tendons, and ligaments, allowing for safer and deeper stretching. Second, it promotes profuse sweating, which practitioners believe aids in toxin elimination. Third, the heat elevates heart rate, creating cardiovascular benefits. Finally, the challenging conditions build mental discipline and focus. The 40% humidity prevents the air from feeling unbearably dry while still allowing sweat to evaporate.

How long is a typical 26+2 class?

Traditional 26+2 classes run 90 minutes. The first 45 minutes consist of the standing series, where 12 postures are each performed twice. The second 45 minutes move to the floor for the remaining 14 postures. Some studios offer 60-minute express versions that move more quickly through the sequence, but the full 90-minute format allows proper time for each posture and adequate rest between challenging sequences.

Is 26+2 Hot Yoga suitable for beginners?

Yes, beginners are welcome and the fixed sequence actually benefits new students. Because the poses are the same every class, beginners learn faster than in styles with varying sequences. Teachers provide modifications for all levels. However, the heat is genuinely challenging. New students should hydrate extensively before class, take breaks whenever needed, and avoid pushing too hard in their first several classes. Most studios recommend trying at least 3-5 classes before deciding if the practice is right for you.

What should I bring to a 26+2 class?

Essential items include:

  • A yoga mat (studios typically rent them if needed)
  • A large towel to cover your entire mat (it will become slippery with sweat)
  • A hand towel for wiping your face
  • At least 32 ounces of water, though more is better
  • Minimal, moisture-wicking clothing (shorts and a tank top or sports bra)
  • A change of clothes for after class

Many studios rent towels and mats, so check with your studio before your first visit.

How often should I practice 26+2?

For beginners, 2-3 classes per week allows for recovery while building familiarity with the sequence. Intermediate practitioners often practice 3-5 times weekly. Dedicated practitioners sometimes complete 30-day challenges, practicing daily for a month. Listen to your body: if you feel exhausted, take rest days. If you feel energized, practice more frequently. The practice becomes more accessible over time as your body adapts to the heat.

What if I feel dizzy or nauseous during class?

Dizziness and nausea can occur, especially in early classes. If you feel unwell, immediately sit or lie down on your mat. Do not leave the room, as the temperature change can make symptoms worse. Take slow, deep breaths through your nose. Sip water. Rest until you feel better. These symptoms usually indicate dehydration or heat sensitivity. With consistent practice and proper hydration, most students find these symptoms diminish or disappear entirely.

Can I practice 26+2 if I am pregnant?

Most medical professionals advise against practicing in extreme heat during pregnancy, as elevated core body temperature can pose risks to fetal development. If you practiced 26+2 before pregnancy, consult your doctor before continuing. Many practitioners switch to prenatal yoga or room-temperature classes during pregnancy and return to the heated room after giving birth and receiving medical clearance. Always prioritize the advice of your healthcare provider.

Why do we practice facing a mirror?

The mirror serves as a tool for concentration and alignment. By watching yourself, you receive immediate feedback on your posture without needing constant teacher adjustment. The mirror also develops focus: by training your gaze on one point (usually your own eyes), you cultivate the concentration necessary to hold challenging postures. Over time, many practitioners find the mirror practice develops self-awareness that extends beyond the studio.
