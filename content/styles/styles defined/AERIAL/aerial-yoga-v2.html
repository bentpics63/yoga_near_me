What is Aerial Yoga?

Aerial Yoga uses a silk hammock suspended from the ceiling to support, deepen, and transform traditional yoga poses. It offers spinal decompression through safe inversions, builds core strength, increases flexibility, and brings playful joy to practice. Beginner-friendly intro classes are available at most aerial studios.

What to Expect in an Aerial Yoga Class

Suspended Silk Hammock

Each student has their own hammock hung at hip height. It supports your weight, enabling inversions and deeper stretches.

Grounded to Flying Progression

Classes start with warm-ups using the hammock at ground level, gradually building to suspended and inverted poses.

Fitted Clothing Required

Wear clothes that cover your armpits and the backs of your knees to prevent friction burns. No zippers or jewelry.

Floating Savasana

Classes end cocooned in the hammock—a floating Savasana that feels like being gently rocked in a hammock.

Aerial Yoga: Elevate Your Practice, Expand Your Perspective

Aerial Yoga invites you to take your practice off the ground and into the air using a silk hammock suspended from the ceiling. This innovative style blends traditional yoga asanas with principles from aerial arts, acrobatics, and Pilates, offering a gravity-defying experience that is both fun and deeply therapeutic.

The hammock becomes a versatile prop—acting as a sling for inversions, a support for balance, a cocoon for rest, and a tool to deepen stretches or add resistance. The result is a practice that challenges the body, expands proprioception, and brings a childlike sense of joy and curiosity.

Aerial Yoga is particularly known for its spinal decompression benefits, allowing practitioners to experience traction and relief from compression in a safe and supported way—especially during inversions. Whether you're seeking physical relief, building strength and flexibility, or simply wanting to experience the joy of flying, Aerial Yoga offers a unique and transformative practice.

The Origins and Evolution of Aerial Yoga

From Circus Arts to Yoga Studio

Aerial Yoga emerged in the early 2000s through the work of performers and movement educators who saw the potential of combining aerial silks with yoga. Pioneers like Christopher Harrison (creator of AntiGravity Fitness) and other aerialists began incorporating traditional yoga poses into the fabric—and a new genre of practice was born.

Since then, various schools and styles have evolved, each with their own approach—ranging from restorative and therapeutic to acro-inspired and fitness-based. What unites them is the use of suspended fabric to support, challenge, and transform traditional yoga postures.

The Hammock as Teacher

Unlike aerial silks used in circus performance (which require significant upper body strength), yoga hammocks are designed to support the whole body, making inversions and suspended poses accessible to practitioners of all fitness levels. The hammock becomes a partner in practice—revealing tension patterns, supporting exploration, and creating space where gravity cannot.

Style and Structure

Style: Playful and Therapeutic

Aerial Yoga blends asana, strength training, acrobatics, and mindfulness in a three-dimensional practice. The silk hammock allows for full-body support in postures, suspension and inversion with reduced joint load, dynamic movement and flow, and playful exploration of shapes and transitions.

Depending on the class, the style may be grounded and gentle (restorative or meditative), strength-focused (core, upper body, balance), flow-based (Vinyasa-like sequences in the air), or inversion-heavy (spinal decompression, flips, and fun).

Structure: Grounded to Flying

A typical Aerial Yoga class may include:

  • Grounded centering and breathwork
  • Warm-up using the hammock at hip or knee height
  • Standing and balancing poses supported by the silk
  • Inversions (aerial Down Dog, Floating Baddha Konasana)
  • Core engagement and strength work
  • Floating Savasana inside the hammock cocoon

Props (mats, blocks, straps) may still be used, but the hammock is the primary tool and anchor point for the entire practice.

Breath and Flow

Breath: The Anchor in Space

Breath plays a vital role in Aerial Yoga:

  • Supporting transitions in and out of suspended poses
  • Calming the nervous system during inversions or novel movements
  • Cultivating body awareness in three-dimensional space
  • Building trust and releasing fear

Teachers often cue steady, grounding breath to help students navigate the sensations of flying, stretching, or inverting. The breath becomes your tether when gravity lets go.

Flow: Dance-Like and Intentional

Flow in Aerial Yoga takes many forms:

  • Graceful and dance-like sequences
  • Strength-focused holds and transitions
  • Playful exploration of shapes
  • Meditative stillness in the cocoon

Transitions often involve unique movement patterns—swivels, drops, wraps, and releases—that aren't found in mat-based yoga. This novelty is part of what makes Aerial Yoga so engaging.

Pace, Focus, and Teacher Role

Pace: Adaptive Intensity

Aerial Yoga classes vary widely in pace:

  • Restorative Aerial: Slow, meditative, focused on deep stretch and nervous system regulation
  • Standard Aerial: Balanced mix of grounded poses, inversions, and strength work
  • Aerial Fitness: Fast-paced, athletic, emphasizing core and upper body conditioning

Teachers typically allow space for acclimation, especially for beginners unfamiliar with inversions or silk navigation.

Focus Areas

Aerial Yoga develops:

  • Spinal decompression: Relief from compression through supported inversions
  • Core strength: Deep stabilization required for suspended movement
  • Flexibility: Gravity-assisted stretching, especially hips and shoulders
  • Proprioception: Enhanced body awareness in three-dimensional space
  • Trust: Learning to let go and trust the hammock

Teacher Role

Aerial Yoga teachers serve as anchors of safety and technical guidance. They are skilled in spotting and modifying poses, creators of joyful and exploratory sequences, and attuned to student confidence and body awareness. A good aerial teacher supports students with step-by-step instruction while holding space for play, focus, and freedom.

Who Is Aerial Yoga For?

Aerial Yoga attracts practitioners seeking something beyond the mat—those ready for adventure, healing, or simply a new perspective. This practice is particularly well-suited for:

Adventure Seekers

Those who crave novelty and want to experience yoga as playful, three-dimensional movement.

Back Pain Sufferers

Those seeking spinal decompression and relief from chronic tension or compression.

Flexibility Seekers

Those wanting to deepen stretches with gravity-assisted support, especially in hips and shoulders.

Core Builders

Those looking to develop deep core strength through functional, suspended movement patterns.

Fear Releasers

Those ready to work through fear and build trust—in the hammock and in themselves.

Beginners Welcome

New students who start with intro classes—the hammock actually makes some poses more accessible.

Who Should Approach with Caution

While Aerial Yoga is highly adaptable, consider consulting a healthcare provider if you:

  • Have vertigo, inner ear issues, or motion sensitivity
  • Have glaucoma or eye pressure concerns
  • Have uncontrolled high blood pressure
  • Are pregnant (seek prenatal-specific classes)
  • Have recent surgeries or herniated discs
  • Have heart conditions or circulatory issues

For those wanting gentler alternatives, consider Restorative Yoga or Yin Yoga for deep relaxation without inversions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Aerial Yoga good for beginners?

Yes, especially in intro-level classes. Teachers provide step-by-step instruction and modifications. The hammock actually makes some poses easier by providing support and reducing joint pressure.

What should I wear to an Aerial Yoga class?

Fitted clothing that covers your armpits and backs of knees to prevent friction burns from the silk. Avoid loose clothing, zippers, buttons, and jewelry that could catch on the fabric.

How much weight can an aerial hammock hold?

Professional hammocks are rated to hold 1,000+ pounds when properly installed. Studios use commercial-grade equipment with appropriate safety margins. Always practice in a properly equipped studio with certified rigging.

Who should avoid Aerial Yoga?

Those with vertigo, glaucoma, uncontrolled high blood pressure, recent surgeries, or certain spinal injuries should consult a healthcare provider before practicing. Pregnant individuals should seek prenatal-specific classes or avoid inversions.

Can Aerial Yoga help with back pain?

Yes, spinal decompression is one of the primary benefits. Inverted poses use gravity to gently lengthen the spine and relieve compression. Many practitioners find significant relief from chronic back tension. Consult your doctor for specific conditions.

Conclusion

Aerial Yoga represents the creative, playful edge of modern yoga practice. Its combination of traditional asana, suspended movement, and accessible inversions creates an experience that is simultaneously physical therapy, moving meditation, and joyful play.

For those who embrace its invitation to fly, the practice offers profound rewards: spinal decompression and relief from chronic tension, strength built through three-dimensional movement, flexibility developed with gravity's assistance, and the confidence that comes from learning to trust.

The hammock becomes your teacher, your supporter, and your playground. It reveals patterns of holding, creates space for release, and invites you to experience your body in entirely new ways.

The journey begins with a willingness to let go, to trust the fabric, and to remember the joy of weightlessness. Find a beginner-friendly class, communicate with your teacher, and allow yourself to fly.

Explore Related Yoga Styles

If you love Aerial's playful challenge, you might also enjoy Acro Yoga for partner-based inversions. For the therapeutic benefits without suspension, try Restorative Yoga. To build the strength that supports aerial practice, explore Power Yoga or Vinyasa.
