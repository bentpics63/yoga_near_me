Kriya Yoga: The Science of Self-Realization

Kriya Yoga stands apart from the physical yoga practices that dominate Western studios. It is not a style of asana practice but rather an ancient meditation technique focused on breathwork, energy control, and direct experience of expanded consciousness.

The word "Kriya" comes from the Sanskrit root "kri," meaning "to do" or "to act"—and in this context refers to a specific action of consciousness using breath and energy. Unlike styles focused on physical fitness, Kriya Yoga's goal is nothing less than self-realization: the direct experience of one's true nature as infinite, blissful consciousness.

In this comprehensive guide, we will explore the lineage and history of Kriya Yoga, examine its unique methodology and techniques, understand its benefits and challenges, and provide a clear path for those drawn to begin this contemplative practice.

Origins and Lineage

From Babaji to the Modern World

Kriya Yoga is described as an ancient technique that was lost for centuries before being revived in modern times. According to tradition, the practice was reintroduced to the world in 1861 when the legendary yogi Mahavatar Babaji initiated Lahiri Mahasaya, a householder and government accountant living in Varanasi, India.

Lahiri Mahasaya (1828-1895) became the first modern teacher of Kriya Yoga, demonstrating that the highest spiritual practices were available to householders—people with jobs, families, and worldly responsibilities—not just renunciates living in caves.

His disciple Sri Yukteswar (1855-1936) continued the lineage, becoming a renowned spiritual teacher and astrologer. Sri Yukteswar's most famous student was Paramahansa Yogananda (1893-1952), who brought Kriya Yoga to the West when he arrived in America in 1920.

Yogananda founded Self-Realization Fellowship (SRF) in 1920 to disseminate the Kriya teachings worldwide. Today, SRF remains one of the primary organizations teaching Kriya Yoga, though several other lineages also trace their roots to Lahiri Mahasaya.

The Living Lineage

The lineage from Babaji through Yogananda is often summarized as: Babaji → Lahiri Mahasaya → Sri Yukteswar → Paramahansa Yogananda. This guru-disciple transmission is considered essential to authentic Kriya practice, as the techniques are traditionally passed through initiation rather than books alone.

Autobiography of a Yogi

In 1946, Paramahansa Yogananda published Autobiography of a Yogi, a spiritual classic that introduced Kriya Yoga to millions of Western readers. The book has never gone out of print and has been translated into over 50 languages.

Autobiography of a Yogi tells the story of Yogananda's spiritual journey from childhood in India through his years of training with Sri Yukteswar and eventually to his mission in America. The book includes accounts of saints, miracles, and spiritual experiences that have captivated readers for decades.

"The Kriya Yogi mentally directs his life energy to revolve, upward and downward, around the six spinal centers... One half-minute of revolution of energy around the sensitive spinal cord of man effects subtle progress in his evolution; that half-minute of Kriya equals one year of natural spiritual unfoldment."

— Paramahansa Yogananda, Autobiography of a Yogi

The book has influenced countless spiritual seekers, including Steve Jobs, who arranged for copies to be given to attendees at his memorial service. For many practitioners, reading Autobiography of a Yogi marked the beginning of their Kriya Yoga journey.

The Kriya Technique

The core Kriya technique is traditionally kept private and transmitted only through initiation. However, its general nature can be described: Kriya is a pranayama technique that circulates energy (prana) up and down the spine, activating the subtle energy centers (chakras) and accelerating spiritual development.

How Kriya Works

According to the teachings, Kriya Yoga works by:

  • Oxygenating the blood: Deep breathing techniques increase oxygen supply to the body and brain
  • Magnetizing the spine: Circulating energy through the spinal centers awakens dormant spiritual potential
  • Withdrawing energy from the senses: Deep meditation allows consciousness to focus inward
  • Accelerating evolution: Regular practice is said to advance spiritual development beyond what occurs naturally

Yogananda described Kriya as "the airplane route to God"—not the only path, but a particularly direct and efficient one for those drawn to it.

Preparatory Techniques

Before learning Kriya proper, students typically learn several preparatory practices:

  • Energization Exercises: A system of 39 exercises to consciously direct energy throughout the body
  • Hong-Sau Technique: A concentration practice using the breath and mantra to calm the mind
  • Om Meditation: Listening for and merging with the cosmic vibration

These practices offer significant benefits on their own while preparing the practitioner for the deeper Kriya technique.

The Practice Path

Kriya Yoga is typically learned through a structured path that unfolds over months or years:

1. Preliminary Study

Most Kriya organizations require preparatory lessons before initiation. Self-Realization Fellowship, for example, offers home-study lessons that cover meditation basics, philosophy, and the preliminary techniques.

2. Establishing Practice

Students are encouraged to establish daily meditation using the preliminary techniques. Consistency matters more than duration—even 15-20 minutes daily builds a foundation.

3. Kriya Initiation

After completing preparatory study (typically 6-12 months), students become eligible for Kriya initiation. This formal ceremony connects the practitioner to the lineage and empowers their practice.

4. Deepening Practice

After initiation, practitioners continue deepening their Kriya practice, often increasing duration and intensity over time. Advanced Kriya techniques may be offered later.

5. Integration

Kriya Yoga emphasizes integrating spiritual practice with daily life. The goal is not escape from the world but living with greater awareness, peace, and purpose.

Benefits of Kriya Yoga

The combination of breathwork, energy circulation, and deep meditation creates a unique set of benefits:

Physical Benefits

Reduced stress, improved breathing, enhanced vitality, better sleep, and potential cardiovascular improvements.

Mental Clarity

Greater mental clarity and concentration, reduced anxiety, increased self-awareness and insight.

Emotional Balance

Deep inner peace and equanimity, reduced emotional reactivity, enhanced creativity and intuition.

Spiritual Awakening

Direct experience of expanded consciousness, connection to inner guidance, progress toward self-realization.

Energy Vitality

Awakening of spiritual potential, increased prana flow, sense of connection to something greater.

Deep Meditation

Systematic techniques for reaching profound meditative states, accessible to dedicated practitioners.

Important Considerations

Kriya Yoga is primarily a spiritual practice, not a physical one. Those seeking physical fitness should explore styles like Vinyasa or Power Yoga. Kriya requires patience, commitment, and readiness for inner work.

Who Is Kriya Yoga For?

Kriya Yoga draws practitioners seeking deep meditation and spiritual development. This practice is particularly well-suited for:

  • Serious meditators wanting systematic, time-tested techniques
  • Spiritual seekers attracted to direct experience over belief
  • Those drawn to Autobiography of a Yogi and Yogananda's teachings
  • Householders seeking practices compatible with work and family life
  • People of all faiths wanting to deepen their spiritual experience
  • Those seeking inner peace through systematic meditation practice

Who Should Explore Other Options

Kriya Yoga may be less suited if you:

  • Seek primarily physical practice (Kriya is meditation-focused—try Vinyasa or Hatha)
  • Want immediate access without preparatory study
  • Are uncomfortable with devotional language or guru lineage
  • Seek quick fixes rather than committed practice
  • Prefer physical movement over stillness (try Kundalini Yoga for a middle path)

How to Begin Your Kriya Yoga Journey

Step 1: Read Autobiography of a Yogi

Paramahansa Yogananda's spiritual classic provides the philosophical foundation for Kriya Yoga. It introduces the lineage, principles, and transformative potential of the practice. Most Kriya paths recommend starting here.

Step 2: Choose a Lineage or Organization

Kriya Yoga is taught through several lineages. Self-Realization Fellowship (SRF), Ananda, and Kriya Yoga International are among the most established. Research their approaches and find one that resonates with your temperament.

Step 3: Begin Preliminary Study

Most Kriya paths require preparatory lessons before initiation. SRF offers home-study lessons; other organizations have their own curricula. These lessons establish the foundation of meditation, concentration, and philosophy.

Step 4: Establish a Daily Meditation Practice

Even before initiation, begin meditating daily. The preliminary techniques—Energization Exercises, Hong-Sau concentration, and Om meditation—prepare you for Kriya and offer immediate benefits.

Step 5: Receive Initiation When Ready

After completing preparatory study (typically 6-12 months), you become eligible for Kriya initiation. This formal transmission connects you to the lineage and empowers your practice. Commitment to regular meditation is expected.

Conclusion

Kriya Yoga offers a profound path for those seeking direct spiritual experience through systematic meditation. Unlike physical yoga styles, it works primarily with breath and energy to accelerate spiritual development and lead practitioners toward self-realization.

For those drawn to its teachings, Kriya Yoga provides a lifetime of deepening practice. The lineage from Babaji through Yogananda has guided millions of seekers, and the tradition continues to thrive through organizations and teachers worldwide.

The path requires patience—months of preparation before initiation, years of consistent practice to deepen. But for those who feel called, the rewards are said to be immeasurable: inner peace, expanded consciousness, and ultimately, direct experience of the divine.

The journey begins with curiosity. Pick up Autobiography of a Yogi. Explore the lineages. Start meditating. The path will unfold from there.

Explore Related Practices

If Kriya Yoga interests you, you may also appreciate Kundalini Yoga for its energy-focused approach, Restorative Yoga for deep relaxation, or Dharma Yoga for its meditative philosophy. Each offers a different doorway into the contemplative dimensions of yoga.
