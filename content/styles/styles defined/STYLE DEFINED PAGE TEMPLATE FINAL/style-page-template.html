What is Ashtanga Yoga

Description

Ashtanga Vinyasa Yoga is a dynamic and disciplined practice that builds strength, flexibility, and inner stillness through a set series of postures linked by breath and movement—rooted in tradition and designed for transformation.

Overview

  • STYLE | Structured, vigorous, and meditative; a moving meditation built on discipline and repetition
  • STRUCTURE | Six progressive series, each with a fixed sequence of postures — most practitioners focus on the Primary Series
  • BREATH | Ujjayi breath throughout; breath drives the movement and creates internal heat (tapas)
  • FLOW | Dynamic and seamless; each posture transitions into the next with a vinyasa (breath-linked movement)
  • PACE | Steady and rhythmic, not slow but not rushed — pace is determined by breath
  • FOCUS | Discipline, purification, concentration (drishti), and self-transformation
  • TEACHER ROLE | Observant guide and hands-on adjuster (especially in Mysore-style); honors lineage and traditional progression
  • VIBE | Focused, quiet, devotional; less about entertainment and more about deep inner work

Benefits of Ashtanga Yoga

Disciplined. Powerful. Transformative.

1

The practice develops lean, balanced strength — using your own body weight to build power, endurance, and control.

2

The Practice

Ashtanga Vinyasa Yoga: Discipline, Flow, and Self-Mastery

Ashtanga Vinyasa Yoga is a structured, breath-centered practice that combines movement, discipline, and meditation into one dynamic flow. Founded by Sri K. Pattabhi Jois, it follows a set series of postures designed to purify the body, focus the mind, and awaken inner energy.

Unlike free-flowing yoga styles, Ashtanga is traditional, precise, and rooted in lineage — you learn one posture at a time and only progress when you're ready. It's a practice of commitment, not convenience — but through that structure comes profound transformation.

This practice is for those who:

  • Want a disciplined routine that builds strength and flexibility
  • Seek mental clarity and inner stillness through repetition
  • Crave the structure of a fixed sequence to deepen their practice
  • Are drawn to traditional yoga with a lineage and philosophy

Ashtanga is more than just a physical workout — it's a moving meditation that cultivates presence, discipline, and self-awareness.

History

Origins and Philosophy

Ashtanga Vinyasa Yoga was brought to modern prominence by Sri K. Pattabhi Jois (1915–2009), who learned it from his teacher, Tirumalai Krishnamacharya, one of the most influential yoga teachers of the 20th century. Jois dedicated his life to teaching the Ashtanga system from his yoga shala in Mysore, India.

The name "Ashtanga" comes from the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali and refers to the eight limbs of yoga, which outline the path to self-realization:

Core Principles

The practice is built on three key pillars:

  • Vinyasa – breath-synchronized movement that creates internal heat
  • Tristhana – three places of attention: posture, breath, and drishti (gaze)
  • Bandhas – internal energy locks that stabilize the body and direct prana

How It Works

Style and Structure

Style

Ashtanga is:

  • Structured and repetitive – the same sequence every time
  • Physically demanding – requires strength, stamina, and focus
  • Meditative and devotional – less about performance, more about presence
  • Traditional – honors lineage, Sanskrit counting, and ritual

The Six Series

Ashtanga consists of six progressive series:

  • Primary Series (Yoga Chikitsa) – "Yoga Therapy"; focuses on alignment, flexibility, and detoxification
  • Intermediate Series (Nadi Shodhana) – "Nerve Purification"; emphasizes backbends and deeper twists
  • Advanced Series A, B, C, D – Challenging sequences for advanced practitioners

Structure of a Typical Class

  • Opening mantra or chant (honoring the lineage)
  • Sun Salutations (Surya Namaskar A and B) – to build heat
  • Standing poses – strength, balance, and grounding
  • Seated poses – flexibility, forward folds, twists
  • Inversions – headstand and shoulderstand
  • Backbends – heart-opening and energy-expanding
  • Closing sequence – lotus poses, inversions, final rest
  • Savasana – final relaxation

Full Ashtanga Primary Series demonstration

Mysore Style vs. Led Class

Mysore Style: A self-paced practice where each student works through the sequence at their own rhythm. The teacher provides one-on-one guidance and adjustments. This is the traditional way to learn Ashtanga.

Led Class: The teacher leads the entire class through the sequence in unison, calling out postures in Sanskrit. Great for beginners or those wanting structure.

Yoga is 99% practice, 1% theory.

The Foundation

Breath and Flow

Breath

Ujjayi breath (victorious breath) is the foundation of Ashtanga:

  • Creates internal heat (tapas) to purify the body
  • Provides a rhythm for movement
  • Deepens concentration and presence
  • Calms the nervous system

Each inhale and exhale is long, steady, and audible — like ocean waves.

Vinyasa (Breath-Linked Movement)

Every movement in Ashtanga is linked to either an inhale or an exhale. This breath-synchronized flow creates:

  • Heat – building internal fire to cleanse the body
  • Rhythm – a steady, meditative pace
  • Focus – no room for distraction when breath guides every move

Flow

Flow in Ashtanga is:

  • Continuous and rhythmic – like a moving meditation
  • Fixed and predictable – the same sequence creates familiarity
  • Challenging but steady – not rushed, but definitely strong

Pace, Focus, and Teacher Role

Pace

The pace is steady, determined by breath — not by the teacher or the clock:

  • Not slow – there's continuous movement
  • Not rushed – breath keeps it sustainable
  • Meditative – rhythm allows the mind to settle

Focus

Ashtanga cultivates focus through:

  • Drishti (gaze points) – each posture has a specific place to look
  • Tristhana – simultaneous awareness of breath, body, and gaze
  • Repetition – doing the same sequence every day reveals subtle layers
  • Bandhas (energy locks) – engaging the core to stabilize and direct energy

Teacher Role

Ashtanga teachers are:

  • Observers and adjusters – especially in Mysore style
  • Keepers of tradition – honoring the lineage
  • Patient and precise – progression happens only when ready
  • Hands-on – adjustments are common (always consensual)

Vibe and Community

The vibe is:

  • Quiet and focused – no chatting or music, just breath
  • Devotional – begins and ends with a chant
  • Humble and disciplined – no flashy tricks, just honest work
  • Community-oriented – especially in Mysore rooms
Is It Right For You?

Who Is Ashtanga Yoga For?

Ideal for:

Athletes & Disciplined Practitioners

Those who thrive on structure, routine, and physical challenge.

People Seeking Mental Clarity

Anyone using movement as meditation to quiet the mind.

Traditional Yoga Students

Those drawn to lineage, philosophy, and devotional practice.

Long-term Practitioners

Students who want depth over variety — mastery through repetition.

People Who Need Heat to Open

Students who find flexibility through warmth rather than force.

May not suit:

  • Those seeking creative, free-flowing sequences
  • People with injuries (unless modified under expert guidance)
  • Students wanting a gentler, restorative practice
  • Anyone resistant to structure or tradition

Conclusion

Ashtanga Vinyasa Yoga is not easy — and that's the point. It's a practice that asks you to show up, do the work, and trust the process. It doesn't promise quick results or instant gratification. Instead, it offers something deeper: a steady path toward transformation.

Through breath, movement, and discipline, Ashtanga teaches you to meet yourself exactly where you are — not where you think you should be. This is yoga as meditation, yoga as purification, yoga as self-mastery.

If you're ready to commit, the mat will meet you there.

Explore Related Yoga Styles

If you love Ashtanga's structure but want more creative freedom, explore Vinyasa Yoga. For alignment precision, try Iyengar Yoga. For fitness intensity, consider Power Yoga. And when you need balance, Yin Yoga offers the perfect counterpoint to Ashtanga's fire.
