What is Ashtanga Yoga?

Ashtanga Yoga is a dynamic, physically demanding practice following a fixed sequence of postures linked by breath (Ujjayi) and movement (vinyasa). Developed by K. Pattabhi Jois in Mysore, India, it builds strength, flexibility, and mental focus through consistent, disciplined practice. The name comes from the Sanskrit "ashta" (eight) and "anga" (limbs), referring to the eight-limbed path of yoga.

What to Expect in Your First Class

Your first Ashtanga class can feel intense. Whether you choose a Mysore-style or led class, expect heat, sweat, and challenge. The practice is demanding but accessible—beginners learn the sequence gradually.

What You'll Do

  • Sun Salutations A & B (Surya Namaskar)
  • Standing sequence (always the same)
  • Primary Series seated poses
  • Closing sequence
  • Final rest (Savasana)

What to Wear & Bring

  • Form-fitting, breathable clothing
  • Your own mat (recommended)
  • Small towel for sweat
  • Water (though traditional practice skips it)
  • Empty stomach (2-3 hours after eating)

How You'll Feel

  • Challenged and energized
  • Hot and sweating
  • Focused and present
  • Accomplished after practice
  • Sore muscles (initially)

The Origins and Philosophy of Ashtanga Yoga

From Mysore to the World

Ashtanga Yoga was developed by K. Pattabhi Jois (1915-2009) in Mysore, India, based on teachings he received from his guru, T. Krishnamacharya. Jois began teaching in 1948 and established the Ashtanga Yoga Research Institute in Mysore, which became a pilgrimage site for serious students worldwide.

The practice spread to the West primarily through students who traveled to Mysore in the 1970s and brought the teachings home. Today, Ashtanga is practiced globally, with authorized teachers and certified teachers maintaining the tradition as taught by Jois.

The Eight Limbs

The name "Ashtanga" references Patanjali's eight-limbed path from the Yoga Sutras:

  1. Yama — Ethical restraints
  2. Niyama — Personal observances
  3. Asana — Physical postures
  4. Pranayama — Breath control
  5. Pratyahara — Sense withdrawal
  6. Dharana — Concentration
  7. Dhyana — Meditation
  8. Samadhi — Absorption/liberation

The Tristhana Method

Ashtanga practice integrates three key elements (tristhana):

  • Ujjayi Pranayama — Victorious breath with audible, oceanic sound
  • Bandhas — Energy locks (mula bandha, uddiyana bandha)
  • Drishti — Focused gaze points for each posture

The Six Series of Ashtanga

Ashtanga Yoga consists of six progressively challenging sequences. Most practitioners spend years, even decades, working through the Primary Series before advancing.

Primary Series (Yoga Chikitsa)

"Yoga Therapy" — The first series detoxifies and aligns the body. It includes standing poses, seated forward bends, twists, and the closing sequence. This is where all students begin and where most practitioners focus their lifetime practice.

Intermediate Series (Nadi Shodhana)

"Nerve Cleansing" — Deepens the work with backbends, arm balances, and hip openers. Opens energy channels (nadis) and works on the nervous system.

Advanced Series A-D (Sthira Bhaga)

"Divine Stability" — Four advanced sequences requiring extraordinary strength, flexibility, and years of dedicated practice. Very few practitioners reach these levels.

Primary Series Structure

  • Opening mantra
  • Sun Salutation A (5 rounds)
  • Sun Salutation B (5 rounds)
  • Standing sequence (~17 poses)
  • Primary Series seated poses (~35 poses)
  • Closing sequence (~16 poses)
  • Savasana & closing mantra

Mysore Style vs. Led Classes

Mysore Style (Traditional)

In Mysore-style classes, students practice the sequence at their own pace while one or more teachers circulate, providing individual adjustments and instruction. This is the traditional method as taught in Mysore, India.

  • Self-paced practice within the group setting
  • Individual attention and adjustments
  • New poses given when ready (teacher's discretion)
  • Develops self-practice and memorization
  • Early morning practice (traditionally starting at 4:30-6am)

Led Classes

In led classes, the teacher calls out poses and counts, and everyone moves together. This format helps students learn the sequence and understand the correct vinyasa count.

  • Teacher-led with Sanskrit counting
  • Everyone moves together
  • Helpful for learning the sequence
  • Less individual attention
  • Traditional Friday practice in some shalas

For beginners: Either format works. Mysore style offers more individual attention; led classes help you learn the sequence structure.

Who Is Ashtanga Yoga For?

Ashtanga attracts practitioners who value structure, discipline, and progressive challenge. It's particularly well-suited for:

Type-A Personalities

Those who thrive on structure, goals, and measurable progress in their practice.

Athletes & Movers

People with existing fitness who want a challenging, athletic yoga practice.

Focus Seekers

Those wanting to develop concentration, presence, and mental discipline.

Routine Lovers

People who appreciate knowing exactly what to expect and doing the same practice daily.

Lifelong Learners

Those committed to long-term study and gradual mastery over years or decades.

Tradition Seekers

Practitioners drawn to lineage, teacher authorization, and traditional methods.

Who Might Prefer Other Practices

Consider other styles if you:

  • Prefer variety and creative sequencing (try Vinyasa)
  • Have injuries that require modifications (try Iyengar for therapeutic approach)
  • Want a gentler, restorative practice (try Yin or Restorative)
  • Find repetition boring or restrictive
  • Can't commit to regular practice (Ashtanga works best with consistency)

How to Start Your Ashtanga Practice

Step 1: Learn Sun Salutations

  • Master Surya Namaskar A first
  • Add Surya Namaskar B when ready
  • Practice until they flow naturally
  • This alone takes weeks to internalize

Step 2: Find a Teacher

  • Look for authorized or certified teachers
  • Try both Mysore and led classes
  • Commit to one teacher initially
  • Be patient with the learning process

Step 3: Master Ujjayi Breath

  • Slight throat constriction
  • Audible, oceanic sound
  • Breath matches movement
  • Never sacrifice breath for pose

Step 4: Build Consistency

  • Start with 3-4 days per week
  • Same time each day if possible
  • Short practice beats no practice
  • Rest on moon days and Saturdays

Remember: In Ashtanga, poses are given progressively by your teacher. Don't rush ahead—master each pose before receiving the next. This patient approach builds a sustainable, injury-free practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between Mysore and led Ashtanga classes?
In Mysore style, students practice at their own pace while teachers provide individual adjustments and instruction. In led classes, the teacher calls out poses and everyone moves together. Mysore is the traditional method; led classes help beginners learn the sequence. Most shalas offer both.
Is Ashtanga Yoga good for beginners?
Yes, though it's physically demanding. Beginners start with Sun Salutations and the first few postures, adding poses gradually as strength and flexibility develop. Mysore classes are ideal for beginners because of the individualized attention—you work at your own pace with teacher guidance.
What is the Primary Series?
The Primary Series (Yoga Chikitsa, meaning "yoga therapy") is the first of six sequences. It includes standing poses, seated forward bends, twists, and a closing sequence—about 75 poses total. Most practitioners spend years refining this series before advancing to Intermediate.
Why do Ashtanga practitioners rest on moon days?
Traditional Ashtanga observes rest on full and new moons. The lunar cycle is believed to affect the body's energy—full moons can bring excess energy leading to injury risk, while new moons can cause fatigue. It's also a time for reflection and to honor natural rhythms.
What is Ujjayi breath and why is it important?
Ujjayi (victorious breath) is created by slightly constricting the throat, producing an audible, oceanic sound. It generates internal heat (tapas), focuses the mind, and helps practitioners maintain rhythm and awareness. The breath is the foundation of the practice—without it, it's just gymnastics.
