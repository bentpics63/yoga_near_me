Dharma Yoga: A Deep Dive into Devotional Practice

Dharma Yoga is a devotional practice founded by Sri Dharma Mittra that integrates classical Hatha yoga, ethical living (particularly ahimsa and compassion), and spiritual teachings aimed at self-realization. Unlike styles focused primarily on physical fitness, Dharma Yoga treats asana as preparation for deeper spiritual practice.

The practice is named after its founder, Sri Dharma Mittra, who has taught in New York City since 1975—making him one of the longest-serving yoga masters in America. Now in his mid-80s, he continues to demonstrate poses that challenge practitioners half his age, embodying the transformative potential of devoted practice.

In this comprehensive guide, we will explore the origins of Dharma Yoga and its founder, examine the famous 908 Asanas poster, break down the unique style and methodology, and help you determine whether this devotional practice is right for your yoga journey.

Origins: Sri Dharma Mittra

From Brazil to New York

Sri Dharma Mittra was born in Brazil in 1939 and came to the United States in the early 1960s. He began his yoga journey in 1964 when he became a devoted disciple of Swami Kailashananda (Yogi Gupta), serving his guru for nearly a decade while deepening his practice and study of classical yoga.

In 1975, Sri Dharma Mittra founded the Dharma Yoga Center in New York City, where he continues to teach today. His approach honors the traditional eight limbs of yoga as outlined in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras, viewing physical practice (asana) as preparation for meditation and spiritual awakening.

The Purpose of Practice

Sri Dharma Mittra teaches that the true purpose of yoga is liberation (samadhi) and union with the divine. Physical poses are important tools, but they serve a larger purpose of preparing the body and mind for deeper practice. This distinguishes Dharma Yoga from styles that focus primarily on fitness.

His teachings emphasize that yoga without ethics is just exercise. The physical practice is inseparable from compassion, non-harming, and service to others.

The 908 Asanas Poster

In 1984, Sri Dharma Mittra completed a remarkable labor of devotion: he photographed himself in 908 different yoga poses and assembled them into a single poster known as the "Master Yoga Chart of 908 Postures."

The project took three years. Working alone with a timer-triggered camera, Sri Dharma Mittra systematically documented the vast range of asana practice. The resulting poster became iconic—you'll find it hanging in yoga studios worldwide, serving as both inspiration and reference.

What the 908 Poster Accomplishes

  • Documents the scope of asana practice: It demonstrates that "yoga poses" extend far beyond the 20-30 shapes most practitioners know
  • Inspires humility: No matter how advanced your practice, there's always more to explore
  • Preserves tradition: Many obscure poses that might otherwise be forgotten are catalogued
  • Embodies devotion: The project itself was an act of seva (selfless service)

Sri Dharma Mittra has said the poster was created as an offering to his guru and to future generations of practitioners. It remains one of the most comprehensive visual records of yoga asana ever created.

Style and Structure

Style: Devotional at Its Core

Dharma Yoga is devotional at its core. While classes include physically demanding poses, the approach is reverent rather than athletic. Practice is treated as an offering—to the teacher, to the tradition, and ultimately to the divine.

The physical practice draws from classical Hatha Yoga, incorporating standing poses, forward folds, backbends, twists, inversions, and arm balances. Sri Dharma Mittra is known for his mastery of advanced poses, but classes are accessible at all levels.

Structure: A Complete Practice

A typical Dharma Yoga class includes:

  • Opening: Chanting, dedication, or moment of centering
  • Warm-up: Psychic development exercises (subtle energy work) and sun salutations
  • Standing poses: Building strength, balance, and grounding
  • Floor sequence: Forward folds, twists, and hip openers
  • Backbends: Opening the heart and cultivating courage
  • Inversions: Often including headstand and shoulderstand
  • Pranayama: Breathing practices to calm and energize
  • Meditation or Yoga Nidra: Deep relaxation and inner exploration
  • Closing: Dedication of merit, gratitude

The balance of elements varies by class level, but Dharma Yoga consistently integrates physical, energetic, and contemplative practices rather than focusing solely on asana.

Ethics and Philosophy

Ahimsa: Non-Harming

Central to Dharma Yoga is ahimsa—non-violence or non-harming. Sri Dharma Mittra teaches that ahimsa is the foundation of all yoga practice. Without compassion, physical accomplishment is meaningless.

This emphasis leads to Dharma Yoga's strong encouragement of vegetarianism (ideally veganism). Sri Dharma Mittra teaches that consuming animal products creates suffering and contradicts the principle of ahimsa. While not enforced, this ethical stance permeates the teachings.

The Yamas and Niyamas

Dharma Yoga explicitly teaches the ethical guidelines outlined in classical yoga:

  • Yamas: Ethical restraints (non-harming, truthfulness, non-stealing, moderation, non-possessiveness)
  • Niyamas: Personal observances (cleanliness, contentment, discipline, self-study, surrender)

These are not treated as add-ons to physical practice but as prerequisites. Sri Dharma Mittra often says that yoga without ethics is just exercise.

Self-Realization

The ultimate goal of Dharma Yoga is self-realization—direct knowledge of one's true nature beyond the limited ego-self. Physical practice prepares the body for meditation; meditation reveals the self; self-knowledge leads to liberation.

This spiritual orientation distinguishes Dharma Yoga from fitness-focused approaches. The practice aims at something beyond flexibility or strength: freedom from suffering and union with the divine.

Who Is Dharma Yoga For?

Dharma Yoga draws practitioners seeking more than physical practice. This style is particularly well-suited for:

Spiritual Seekers

Those wanting yoga's full philosophical context and a path toward self-realization.

Devotional Practitioners

Those drawn to the guru-student relationship and practice as an offering.

Ethical Vegetarians

Those seeking a practice that aligns with values of non-harming and compassion.

Meditators

Those wanting physical practice to support and deepen their sitting meditation.

Tradition Seekers

Anyone curious about yoga's classical roots beyond modern fitness trends.

Experienced Practitioners

Those ready to deepen beyond asana into yoga's broader spiritual dimensions.

Who Should Explore Other Options

Dharma Yoga may be less suited if you:

  • Prefer purely physical fitness without spiritual elements (try Power Yoga)
  • Are uncomfortable with devotional language or chanting
  • Want high-energy, music-driven classes (try Vinyasa)
  • Prefer precise alignment instruction without philosophy (try Iyengar)
  • Need a completely secular practice environment

How to Start Your Dharma Yoga Practice

Beginning Dharma Yoga involves both physical and attitudinal preparation:

Step 1: Explore the Ethical Foundation

Before your first class, reflect on ahimsa (non-harming) and compassion. Dharma Yoga treats ethics as foundational—not optional. Consider how these principles might apply to your own life.

Step 2: Start with a Level 1 Class

Look for "Dharma Yoga Level 1" or "Dharma Yoga Basics." These classes move at an accessible pace and explain the philosophical context alongside the physical poses.

Step 3: Come with an Open Heart

Dharma Yoga includes devotional elements—chanting, dedication of practice, and spiritual teachings. Approach with openness rather than judgment. You don't need to share every belief to benefit from the practice.

Step 4: Learn the Signature Elements

Dharma Yoga includes poses you might recognize—Sun Salutations, standing poses, backbends—but also emphasizes psychic development exercises, pranayama, and deep relaxation (Yoga Nidra).

Step 5: Consider the Broader Lifestyle

Sri Dharma Mittra encourages vegetarianism, self-study, and service to others. These aren't prerequisites for practice, but understanding them helps you engage more fully with the teachings over time.

Conclusion

Dharma Yoga offers something increasingly rare in modern yoga: a practice that honors the tradition's spiritual depth while remaining physically rigorous. Sri Dharma Mittra's five decades of teaching demonstrate that ethics and asana are inseparable—that compassion is the foundation upon which all practice rests.

For those seeking more than fitness, Dharma Yoga provides a path. The practice integrates physical challenge with ethical inquiry, meditation with movement, personal development with service to others. It asks practitioners to consider not just how they move, but how they live.

Whether you're drawn to the iconic 908 Asanas poster, curious about yoga's classical roots, or seeking a practice aligned with vegetarian values, Dharma Yoga offers depth that rewards sustained engagement. The journey begins with an open heart and a willingness to explore beyond the physical.

Explore Related Yoga Styles

If you appreciate Dharma Yoga's traditional roots, explore Hatha Yoga for classical asana or Kundalini Yoga for energy work. For more emphasis on meditation, consider Kriya Yoga. For alignment focus with philosophical depth, Iyengar Yoga offers a complementary approach.
