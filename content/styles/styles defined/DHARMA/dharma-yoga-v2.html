Dharma Yoga

A classical practice rooted in ethics, devotion, and the transformative power of disciplined asana from the master who photographed 908 poses

By Lisa Marie Updated Jan 27, 2026 14 min read
Quick Answer

What is Dharma Yoga?

Dharma Yoga is a classical yoga system founded by Sri Dharma Mittra in New York City that seamlessly weaves together Hatha, Raja, and Karma yoga traditions. Distinguished by its unwavering emphasis on ethical foundations (particularly ahimsa and the yamas/niyamas), challenging inversions and backbends, and devotional practice dedicated to something higher than oneself, Dharma Yoga offers a complete path for physical mastery and spiritual awakening. The practice is immortalized in the legendary Master Yoga Chart—908 postures photographed by Dharma himself—which stands as one of yoga's most comprehensive visual references.

Walking into a Dharma Yoga class feels different from the moment you cross the threshold. There's an atmosphere of reverence here—not religiosity, but genuine respect for what you're about to undertake. The practice you're entering has been shaped by decades of one teacher's dedicated investigation into what yoga can become when ethics and devotion stand equal to physical practice.

Sri Dharma Mittra didn't set out to create a "style" of yoga. He set out to live yoga completely, and what emerged from that commitment became a distinct approach that has touched hundreds of thousands of practitioners since he began teaching in 1967. When you practice Dharma Yoga, you're connecting to a living lineage—Dharma himself, now in his eighties, still teaches and demonstrates poses that would challenge practitioners half his age.

What makes this practice transformative isn't just the challenging inversions or the precise backbends (though there are plenty of both). It's the integration of ethical principles into every aspect of practice. Dharma Yoga reminds us that the postures are preparation—powerful preparation—for the deeper work of meditation and self-realization. The body becomes capable; the mind becomes steady; something beyond both begins to reveal itself.

"The main thing in yoga is to become a good human being. That's it. Then everything else—all the poses, all the breathing, all the meditation—these become tools to help you be kind, to help you stop hurting others, to help you see yourself in everyone." — Sri Dharma Mittra

This isn't a practice that lets you coast on good intentions. The physical demands are real—headstands held for minutes, backbends that ask everything of your spine, arm balances that require both strength and surrender. Yet the rigor never becomes about ego achievement. Every challenging pose is offered as devotion, as a way of preparing the vessel for something greater than personal accomplishment.

For those ready to embrace both the physical challenge and the ethical depth, Dharma Yoga offers a complete system—one that can genuinely transform not just your body, but your relationship to yourself and everyone around you.

What to Expect in a Dharma Yoga Class

Every class element serves the larger purpose of ethical refinement and spiritual awakening

Opening Invocation

Classes begin with chanting and dedication, setting an intention that transcends personal goals. You're invited to offer your practice to something greater—a teacher, a cause, all beings. This isn't optional decoration; it's foundational to the Dharma approach.

Shiva Namaskar

Dharma's signature sun salutation variation flows through poses that honor Lord Shiva. More expansive than traditional Surya Namaskar, this sequence builds heat while incorporating poses like Warrior I and prayer twists that prepare the body for deeper work.

Standing Sequences

Extended standing pose series build stamina and leg strength. Warriors, triangles, and balancing poses are held longer than you might expect, developing the endurance needed for inversions. Precise alignment cues protect joints while challenging muscles.

Inversions Emphasis

Expect significant time upside down. Headstand, shoulderstand, and their variations aren't afterthoughts—they're central to the practice. Dharma Yoga builds progressively toward extended inversions, viewing them as essential for reversing energy flow and calming the mind.

Deep Backbends

Heart-opening is literal here. Wheel pose, camel, and advanced variations like Kapotasana appear regularly. The emphasis on backbends reflects Dharma's teaching that opening the heart center is essential for developing compassion and receiving grace.

Psychic Development

Dharma Yoga includes practices aimed at developing intuition and subtle awareness. Visualization exercises, concentration techniques, and guidance on directing prana distinguish this from purely physical approaches. The goal extends beyond fitness.

Pranayama Practice

Breath work isn't rushed. Expect dedicated time for kapalabhati, nadi shodhana, and other classical techniques. Dharma emphasizes breath control as the bridge between physical practice and meditation, teaching specific techniques to channel life force.

Extended Meditation

Classes conclude with genuine meditation, not just brief relaxation. You'll be guided through concentration practices, sometimes including visualization of light or chakra awareness. This is where the physical preparation reveals its purpose.

The Journey of Sri Dharma Mittra

Understanding the man illuminates the practice. Sri Dharma Mittra's path from Brazilian wrestler to one of yoga's most respected masters reveals how discipline, devotion, and decades of practice crystallize into authentic teaching.

1939

Born in Pirapora, Brazil

Sri Dharma Mittra was born into a large family in rural Brazil. As a young man, he developed discipline through bodybuilding and wrestling, unknowingly preparing his body for the yoga practice to come.

1958

Discovers Yoga

While serving in the Brazilian Air Force, Dharma encountered yoga through a book. The teachings resonated immediately, and he began practicing on his own with dedicated intensity.

1964

Arrives in New York City

Dharma moved to NYC seeking deeper yoga study. He worked odd jobs while searching for an authentic teacher, eventually finding his guru.

1966

Meets Swami Kailashananda

Dharma became a disciple of Swami Kailashananda (Yogi Gupta), beginning nine years of intensive study. He served his guru completely—cleaning, cooking, and practicing for hours daily—while receiving classical yoga transmission.

1967

Begins Teaching

At his guru's direction, Dharma began leading classes while continuing his own intensive practice. His teaching integrated everything he was learning with his natural gift for demonstrating poses.

1975

Opens Dharma Yoga Center

Dharma established his own center in New York City, creating a space dedicated to classical yoga practice. The center became a landmark for serious practitioners seeking authentic teaching.

1984

Creates the Master Yoga Chart

Over two years, Dharma photographed himself in 908 yoga postures, compiling them into the legendary Master Yoga Chart. He cut and pasted each photo by hand, creating what remains one of yoga's most comprehensive visual references.

2000s-Present

Global Influence Expands

Dharma's Life Force Teacher Training programs have certified thousands of teachers worldwide. Now in his mid-eighties, he continues teaching daily, demonstrating poses that inspire practitioners decades younger.

6 Key Benefits of Dharma Yoga

A practice that transforms body, mind, and ethical consciousness

01

Complete Physical Development

The integration of inversions, backbends, and sustained holds builds comprehensive strength, flexibility, and endurance. Unlike styles that emphasize one quality, Dharma Yoga develops the whole body while teaching it to work as a unified system.

02

Ethical Grounding

Regular engagement with yamas and niyamas translates practice into daily life. Students report becoming more aware of their impact on others, making choices that create less harm. The mat becomes training ground for being a better person.

03

Mental Resilience

Holding challenging poses while maintaining calm breath builds psychological strength that transfers off the mat. When your headstand becomes steady, life's difficulties seem more manageable. Physical challenge becomes mental training.

04

Deepened Meditation Capacity

The physical practice genuinely prepares the body for stillness. After working through inversions and backbends, sitting becomes more accessible. Students often find their meditation practice improves significantly alongside their asana practice.

05

Connection to Living Lineage

Practicing a system with a clear, living source provides accountability and authenticity. Sri Dharma Mittra still teaches, answers questions, and demonstrates what decades of practice can become. This isn't abstract philosophy—it's embodied example.

06

Subtle Energy Awareness

The combination of pranayama, visualization, and physical practice develops sensitivity to prana and internal states. Many practitioners report increased intuition, more vivid dreams, and a felt sense of energy that guides their practice and life.

Signature Elements of Dharma Yoga

These distinctive practices set Dharma Yoga apart and reveal its comprehensive approach to transformation.

The Master Yoga Chart

In 1984, Sri Dharma Mittra photographed himself performing 908 yoga postures, then hand-assembled these images into a comprehensive chart. This labor of love took two years and remains one of yoga's most complete visual references. The chart isn't mere documentation—it's a teaching tool showing possibilities for the human body when practice is sustained over decades. Many students hang it in their practice spaces as both inspiration and reference.

Shiva Namaskar

Dharma's signature sun salutation variation honors Lord Shiva, the archetypal yogi. More elaborate than traditional Surya Namaskar, the sequence includes Warrior poses, prayer twists, and specific breathing patterns. It builds substantial heat while maintaining devotional focus. Learning Shiva Namaskar marks entry into authentic Dharma practice.

Maha Mudra

The "Great Seal" practice combines posture, breath retention, and bandhas (energetic locks) to direct prana through the central channel. Dharma teaches specific versions of this classical practice, viewing it as essential for awakening deeper energies. Maha Mudra appears in most classes once students understand its components.

Life Force Series

The structured class format progresses through levels (I-IV) that systematically build capacity. Life Force I introduces fundamentals; Life Force II adds complexity and longer holds; Dharma III and IV present advanced inversions and backbends. This clear progression allows students to develop safely while always having something to grow toward.

Psychic Development

Unlike purely physical approaches, Dharma Yoga includes practices aimed at developing intuition and subtle perception. Visualization exercises, concentration on internal points, and specific techniques for directing awareness distinguish this as more than fitness. Dharma views these capacities as natural results of sustained ethical practice.

Ahimsa as Foundation

Non-violence isn't just mentioned—it's the first requirement. Dharma strongly encourages vegetarianism, viewing it as essential for purifying the body and avoiding karmic complications. This emphasis on ahimsa extends to speech, thought, and all aspects of life. The physical practice rests on this ethical foundation.

Typical Life Force Class Structure

While specific sequences vary by teacher and level, most Dharma Yoga classes follow this general arc.

0-10 min

Opening & Dedication

Class begins with centering, often in a comfortable seated position. Chanting may include Om and other mantras. Students are invited to dedicate their practice to something beyond personal benefit—a teacher, a cause, all beings. This sets the devotional tone that distinguishes Dharma Yoga.

10-25 min

Shiva Namaskar & Warm-Up

Multiple rounds of Shiva Namaskar build heat progressively. The pace may quicken as the body warms. Additional preparatory poses target hips, shoulders, and spine—areas that need opening for later inversions and backbends.

25-45 min

Standing Sequences

Extended standing pose series develop stamina and leg strength. Warriors, triangles, half-moon, and standing balances are held longer than in many vinyasa classes. This builds the foundation for inversion stability. Expect your legs to work.

45-60 min

Inversions & Arm Balances

The heart of many Dharma classes. Headstand variations, shoulderstand sequences, and arm balances receive significant attention. Teachers offer modifications, but the expectation is that students work toward full expressions over time. Extended holds (3-5 minutes) are common.

60-70 min

Backbends & Heart Openers

Wheel pose, camel, and deeper backbends follow inversions. The sequencing is intentional—inversions prepare the spine for opening. Counter-poses and twists integrate the backbend work before moving toward stillness.

70-80 min

Pranayama & Seated Practice

Dedicated breath work follows physical practice. Kapalabhati, nadi shodhana, and other classical techniques prepare the mind for meditation. Maha Mudra may appear here, sealing the physical practice before stillness.

80-90 min

Meditation & Closing

Genuine meditation time, not just brief savasana. Guided visualization, concentration practices, or silent sitting may be offered. Classes close with additional chanting and often the blessing "Hari Om." Students leave having touched stillness.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about Dharma Yoga practice

What is Dharma Yoga?

Dharma Yoga is a classical yoga system founded by Sri Dharma Mittra that integrates Hatha, Raja, and Karma yoga traditions. It emphasizes ethical living (yamas and niyamas), challenging physical postures including inversions and backbends, and devotional practice. The style is known for its Life Force series classes and the Master Yoga Chart featuring 908 poses. Unlike purely physical approaches, Dharma Yoga views the postures as preparation for meditation and self-realization.

Is Dharma Yoga suitable for beginners?

Yes, Dharma Yoga offers classes for all levels. While the style is known for advanced poses, beginner-friendly Life Force I classes provide accessible entry points with emphasis on proper alignment and ethical foundations. Teachers offer modifications, and students progress through levels (I-IV) as their practice develops. That said, some basic fitness and body awareness help—complete beginners may benefit from a few months of gentler practice first.

What is the Master Yoga Chart?

The Master Yoga Chart is a comprehensive poster created by Sri Dharma Mittra in 1984, featuring 908 yoga postures. Dharma photographed himself in each pose, then cut and pasted the images by hand to create what many consider the most complete visual reference of yoga asanas ever compiled. It serves as both an educational tool and an inspiration for practitioners worldwide, demonstrating what decades of dedicated practice can achieve.

Why is vegetarianism emphasized in Dharma Yoga?

Vegetarianism in Dharma Yoga stems from the core principle of ahimsa (non-violence), the first and most important yama. Sri Dharma Mittra teaches that causing harm to animals for food creates karmic obstacles to spiritual progress. A vegetarian diet is considered essential for purifying the body and mind, enabling deeper meditation and ethical living. While not absolutely required to practice, it's strongly encouraged as students progress.

What is the Life Force series?

The Life Force series is Dharma Yoga's signature class format, designed to systematically awaken and direct prana (life energy) through the body. Classes include Shiva Namaskar (sun salutation variation), standing poses, inversions, backbends, pranayama, and meditation. The series ranges from Level I (beginner) to Level IV (advanced), with Maha Sadhana being an extended 3-hour practice for committed students. Each level builds on the previous, providing clear progression.

How is Dharma Yoga different from Ashtanga or Vinyasa?

While all three styles include flowing sequences, Dharma Yoga places greater emphasis on ethical foundations, devotional practice, and psychic development. Unlike Ashtanga's fixed sequences, Dharma classes vary while maintaining structural consistency. The practice includes unique elements like Shiva Namaskar, Maha Mudra, and extended inversions not typically found in Vinyasa classes. Meditation receives more time, and the devotional dedication at the beginning sets a different tone than fitness-focused styles.

What should I bring to a Dharma Yoga class?

Bring a yoga mat, comfortable clothing that allows full range of movement, and water if you like. Studios typically provide props (blocks, straps, blankets), but you can bring your own. Avoid eating heavily for 2-3 hours before class—the inversions and backbends are more comfortable with a lighter stomach. Arrive a few minutes early to settle in and set your intention.

Is Sri Dharma Mittra still teaching?

Yes, remarkably, Sri Dharma Mittra continues to teach regularly at the Dharma Yoga Center in New York City and leads workshops and trainings worldwide. Now in his mid-eighties, he still demonstrates poses that challenge practitioners decades younger. Experiencing his teaching directly remains possible for those who seek it—a rare opportunity to learn from a living master.

What is Maha Sadhana?

Maha Sadhana ("great practice") is an extended 3-hour Dharma Yoga session that includes the full range of practice elements: extensive asana sequences, pranayama, mudras, meditation, and often chanting or philosophical teaching. It's designed for committed students and offers a deeper immersion than standard classes. Many practitioners find it transformative, allowing time for states that shorter classes can't access.

Where can I find Dharma Yoga classes?

The Dharma Yoga Center in New York City is the primary source, but certified Life Force teachers teach worldwide. Look for teachers who completed the Dharma Yoga Life Force Teacher Training. Online classes are also available through the Dharma Yoga center. Use our studio directory to find certified Dharma Yoga teachers in your area.
