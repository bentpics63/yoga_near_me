Inversions Defined

vee-pah-REE-tah STEE-tee (inverted position)

An inversion is any yoga pose where your heart is higher than your head. This category spans from gentle poses like Downward Dog to challenging ones like Headstand—all sharing the common quality of flipping your usual relationship with gravity.

What Counts as an Inversion?

The simple definition: any pose where your head is below your heart. By this measure, inversions range from Standing Forward Fold (technically an inversion!) to full Headstand. Some teachers define inversions more strictly as poses where the hips are above the heart and the head is below both, which narrows the category to poses like Shoulder Stand, Headstand, and Handstand.

The broader definition is more useful for understanding what inversions offer: a change in the body's relationship to gravity. When you invert, blood flows differently, the heart works differently, and your perception shifts. Even a simple forward fold provides some of these effects.

Traditional yoga categorizes inversions as a distinct group—sometimes called viparita sthiti (inverted positions)—recognizing their unique effects on the body and mind.

Common Yoga Inversions

Gentle Inversions

  • Downward-Facing Dog — Heart above head, weight distributed between hands and feet
  • Standing Forward Fold (Uttanasana) — Head hangs below the heart with feet grounded
  • Wide-Legged Forward Fold — Similar to Uttanasana with a wider base
  • Legs Up the Wall (Viparita Karani) — Restorative, with legs elevated and back on the ground

Moderate Inversions

  • Bridge Pose — Heart lifted above head while shoulders stay grounded
  • Dolphin Pose — Like Downward Dog but on forearms; prepares for Headstand
  • Plow Pose — Legs over head, resting on shoulders

Full Inversions

  • Shoulderstand — The "queen of poses"; body vertical, weight on shoulders
  • Headstand — The "king of poses"; body vertical, weight on forearms and crown of head
  • Forearm Stand (Pincha Mayurasana) — Body vertical, weight entirely on forearms
  • Handstand (Adho Mukha Vrksasana) — Body vertical, weight on hands
Starting with inversions: If you're new to inversions, begin with gentle versions and work up gradually. Legs Up the Wall offers many inversion benefits with minimal risk. From there, build strength and body awareness before attempting full inversions like Headstand or Handstand.

Why Practice Inversions?

  • Changes blood flow patterns — Being upside down gives the heart a different workload and brings blood to the upper body more easily
  • Stimulates the lymphatic system — The lymphatic system relies on movement and gravity changes; inversions support this flow
  • Builds upper body and core strength — Supporting your body weight upside down requires significant strength
  • Shifts perspective — Literally seeing the world upside down can refresh mental patterns and encourage new viewpoints
  • Challenges fear and builds confidence — Learning to trust yourself inverted develops courage that extends beyond the mat
  • Calms the nervous system — Many inversions (especially supported ones) activate the parasympathetic response

Who Should Avoid Inversions?

While gentle inversions like Legs Up the Wall are safe for most people, full inversions carry considerations:

  • Neck injuries — Headstand, Shoulderstand, and Plow place load on the cervical spine
  • High blood pressure — Being inverted increases blood pressure to the head
  • Glaucoma or eye conditions — Increased intraocular pressure may be problematic
  • Heart conditions — Consult a doctor before inverting with cardiac issues
  • Pregnancy — Generally avoided, especially without an established practice
  • Menstruation — Some traditions recommend avoiding inversions during this time; others don't. Listen to your body

Inversions in Practice

Different yoga styles approach inversions differently. Iyengar Yoga emphasizes alignment and uses props to make inversions accessible. Ashtanga Yoga includes a specific inversion sequence at the end of practice. Vinyasa classes might include inversions throughout. Restorative practices focus on supported versions like Legs Up the Wall.

In sequencing, inversions often appear toward the end of practice, after the body is warm and before final relaxation. They're typically followed by gentle counterposes before Savasana.
