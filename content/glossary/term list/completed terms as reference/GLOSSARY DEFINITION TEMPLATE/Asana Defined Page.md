      \<h2\>Short Definition\</h2\>  
      \<p\>The Sanskrit word \<strong\>asana\</strong\> means "seat" or "posture." In modern yoga, asana refers to the physical poses practiced in classâ€”from simple grounding shapes like \<strong\>Mountain Pose\</strong\> to dynamic sequences like \<strong\>Sun Salutations\</strong\>. Each pose is held with mindful attention to breath and alignment.\</p\>

      \<h2\>What Asana Means in a Yoga Class\</h2\>  
      \<p\>In class, "asana" is everything you physically do with your body: standing, balancing, twisting, folding, backbending, relaxing. Teachers cue alignment, breath, and transitions so poses support your body rather than strain it.\</p\>

      \<p\>For teachers and studio owners, asana is the accessible entry point into yoga. It's where students build confidence, learn body awareness, and discover what styles resonate with them.\</p\>

      \<h2\>Why Asana Matters\</h2\>  
      \<ul\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Builds strength, mobility, and balance\</strong\> safely and sustainably through progressive practice\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Helps students connect breath with movement\</strong\> for greater focus and mind-body integration\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Supports long-term joint and spine health\</strong\> through mindful alignment and functional movement\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Improves focus and emotional regulation\</strong\> by training present-moment awareness\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Forms the physical foundation\</strong\> for all yoga styles and traditions\</li\>  
      \</ul\>

      \<h2\>Asana Across Different Yoga Styles\</h2\>  
      \<p\>Each yoga style approaches asana differently. Understanding these variations helps students find the practice that best serves their goals:\</p\>

      \<ul\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Hatha Yoga\</strong\> â€“ Breaks down poses step-by-step with longer holds, focusing on alignment and technique\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Vinyasa Flow\</strong\> â€“ Links poses together in flowing, dynamic sequences synchronized with breath\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Iyengar Yoga\</strong\> â€“ Uses props and precise alignment cues to refine postures and support all bodies\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Yin Yoga\</strong\> â€“ Focuses on long, passive holds (3-5 minutes) to access deeper tissues and promote relaxation\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Power Yoga\</strong\> â€“ Emphasizes strength and intensity through faster-paced, dynamic sequences\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Restorative Yoga\</strong\> â€“ Uses props to support gentle, supported poses for deep relaxation and healing\</li\>  
      \</ul\>

      \<div class="ynm-term-note"\>  
        \<strong\>Tip for Beginners:\</strong\> There's no "perfect" asana. Every body is different, and the goal is to honor your unique body while building strength and awareness. Listen to your teacher's cues, use modifications when needed, and remember that your practice is about progress, not perfection.  
      \</div\>

      \<h2\>Common Asanas You'll Encounter\</h2\>  
      \<p\>Most yoga classes include foundational poses that appear across different styles. These are great entry points for building confidence:\</p\>

      \<ul\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Mountain Pose (Tadasana)\</strong\> â€“ Standing foundation pose for alignment and grounding\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Child's Pose (Balasana)\</strong\> â€“ Gentle resting pose for relaxation and hamstring stretching\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Downward Dog (Adho Mukha Svanasana)\</strong\> â€“ Foundational inversion and hamstring stretch\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Warrior Poses (Virabhadrasana I, II, III)\</strong\> â€“ Standing poses that build strength and stability\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Tree Pose (Vrksasana)\</strong\> â€“ Balance pose for focus and leg strength\</li\>  
        \<li\>\<strong\>Corpse Pose (Savasana)\</strong\> â€“ Final relaxation pose for integration and restoration\</li\>  
      \</ul\>
